# One CIA Coup Changed the Middle East Forever

In 1953, the CIA spent roughly $1 million to overthrow a democratically elected prime minister. The cost of that decision — measured in hostage crises, proxy wars, nuclear standoffs, and assassinations — is still being paid seventy years later, by both sides.

Most foreign policy analysis treats the Iran-US rivalry as a contemporary problem. It is not. It is the longest-running strategic failure in American diplomatic history — and the most consequential intelligence blowback in the modern era.

### The Pattern No One Talks About

In *The Persian Grudge: How Iran and America Became Mortal Enemies*, journalist Michael Rodriguez identifies a pattern that repeats with almost mechanical precision across 120 years:

1. **Intervention** — an outside power intervenes in Iran to secure its interests
2. **Short-term success** — the intervention works, temporarily
3. **Blowback** — the intervention generates a backlash more dangerous than the original problem
4. **Escalation** — the intervener responds with more intervention

The British wanted cheap oil in 1901. They got it — and triggered the nationalization crisis of 1953. Washington wanted a compliant Cold War ally. They installed the Shah — and got the Islamic Revolution. The Reagan administration wanted to contain Iran by arming Saddam Hussein. That worked until Saddam invaded Kuwait.

Each "solution" manufactured the next crisis.

### Three Things Every Professional Should Know

**1. The economics are staggering.** Between 1913 and 1951, the Anglo-Iranian Oil Company extracted over $3 billion in revenue from Iranian oil fields while paying Iran a fraction. Adjusted for inflation, that single concession transferred the equivalent of tens of billions from Iran to British coffers. The resentment this created was not ideological — it was financial.

**2. The intelligence failure is structural.** According to declassified NSA documents, Washington's assessment of Iran as its "most reliable Middle Eastern partner" was based on intelligence filtered almost entirely through the Shah's own security apparatus (SAVAK). When the Revolution came, the CIA was caught flat-footed — not because they lacked data, but because their data source was the regime itself.

**3. The proxy war model persists.** The doctrine Qasem Soleimani perfected — asymmetric warfare through proxy forces — did not die with him on that Baghdad highway in 2020. It has been adopted, adapted, and deployed across Lebanon, Yemen, Iraq, and Syria. Understanding how this doctrine emerged is essential for anyone analyzing Middle Eastern security today.

### Why This Matters Now

The 2015 nuclear deal (JCPOA) was the most significant diplomatic achievement between Iran and America since 1979. Its destruction three years later — and the failure to replace it — has left both nations closer to military confrontation than at any point since the hostage crisis.
