# INTRODUCTION
## The Room Where It Happens

June 6, 2024. A Thursday. The kind of warm Madrid evening where tourists drink Tempranillo on the Gran Vía and argue about whether to visit the Prado or the Reina Sofía. Three kilometers south, at the Hotel Eurostars Suites Mirasierra, something else entirely was happening.

One hundred and thirty of the most powerful people on Earth were checking in.

They arrived in black sedans with tinted windows. Spanish National Police had cordoned off the surrounding streets since dawn — a perimeter so tight that residents of the adjacent apartment blocks needed government-issued passes to reach their own front doors. No press conferences were scheduled. No journalists were invited. Recording devices were prohibited. Minutes would not be taken — or if they were, they would never be released to the public. The hotel's regular guests had been quietly relocated to other properties weeks in advance, their reservations canceled with apologetic emails and generous refunds.

Inside: CEOs of companies you use every day. Sitting prime ministers. Former intelligence chiefs. The head of NATO. Central bankers whose decisions move trillions — people whose Monday morning phone calls can crash a currency or green-light a trade war. Your tax dollars, your trade agreements, your economic future were almost certainly on the agenda somewhere between the opening remarks and the Ibérico ham. But you'll never know for certain, because nobody in that room will ever tell you what was said.

Outside, three blocks beyond the police cordon, a few hundred protesters gathered with hand-painted signs. "WE KNOW WHAT YOU'RE DOING," read one. "BILDERBERG = NEW WORLD ORDER," read another. A man in a tinfoil hat — there's always one — shouted into a megaphone about the end of sovereignty. A woman in a "RESIST" t-shirt live-streamed to her 340 followers on X, narrating the arrival of black sedans she couldn't see behind the concrete barriers. Spanish police stood in a loose line, looking bored. They'd done this before. The Bilderberg security contract pays well.

Here's the thing. The protesters were wrong.

Not wrong about the meeting existing. That part was public knowledge — the Bilderberg Group has maintained an official website since 2010 and publishes its participant list every year. In 2024, that list included the King of the Netherlands, the Secretary General of NATO, the CEO of Goldman Sachs, and the director of the National Security Agency. All confirmed. All verifiable. You can look it up right now.

No, the protesters were wrong about something more fundamental. They didn't know what was being discussed. Nobody outside that hotel did. And that gap — between what we know about who attends and what we don't know about what they decide — is the crack in the foundation of democratic accountability that this entire book will pry open.

---

Think about that for a moment. Really sit with it.

An organization that has met annually since 1954 — seventy years — openly publishes the names of its attendees but refuses, categorically and without exception, to reveal what those attendees discuss. If the conversations are innocent, why the secrecy? And if they're not innocent, why publish the names at all?

This is a paradox that should bother you whether you're a card-carrying conspiracy theorist or a card-carrying skeptic. Because both camps — and I've spent years talking to people in each — tend to get it spectacularly wrong.

---

The numbers tell a story worth hearing. A 2024 AP-NORC poll found that 61 percent of Americans believe "a group of people in government are working against us." Not a fringe minority. Not the tinfoil-hat crowd. Sixty-one percent. A separate Pew Research Center survey from the same year showed that public trust in the federal government had dropped to 22 percent — the lowest sustained level since Pew began tracking the question in 1958. And a 2023 YouGov study found that 52 percent of Americans believe "secret groups" exercise significant influence over political decisions.

Half the country.

Now, you could dismiss those numbers as evidence of mass delusion — the byproduct of social media echo chambers and too many late-night YouTube rabbit holes. Plenty of commentators do exactly that. And some of them have a point. QAnon, flat earth theories, 5G mind-control fantasies — the internet has produced a carnival of unfounded beliefs that would have embarrassed a medieval peasant.

But here's where it gets uncomfortable. Because some of the most outlandish-sounding claims of the past century turned out to be true. Not theoretically true. Not metaphorically true. Documentarily, provably, declassified-files-sitting-in-the-National-Archives true.

---

Let me give you some examples. And I want you to notice your own reaction as you read them, because your reaction will tell you something important about the assumptions you're bringing to this book.

MK-Ultra. From 1953 to 1973, the Central Intelligence Agency ran a covert program that dosed American citizens with LSD — without their knowledge or consent — as part of mind-control experiments. The program operated across more than eighty institutions, including Harvard, Stanford, and dozens of hospitals and prisons. Test subjects included prisoners, mental patients, and at least one CIA employee — Frank Olson — who fell from a thirteenth-floor hotel window in New York City under circumstances his family spent sixty years trying to unravel. In 1973, CIA Director Richard Helms ordered all MK-Ultra files destroyed. He almost succeeded. But in 1977, a Freedom of Information Act request uncovered 20,000 documents that had been misfiled in a financial records archive. The Church Committee — a Senate investigation chaired by Idaho Senator Frank Church — confirmed the program's existence in 1975.

That wasn't a conspiracy theory. That was a conspiracy.

COINTELPRO. From 1956 to 1971, the FBI operated a counter-intelligence program designed to "disrupt, discredit, and neutralize" domestic political organizations that Director J. Edgar Hoover considered subversive. Targets included the Communist Party USA, the civil rights movement, the Black Panther Party, the American Indian Movement, feminist organizations, and anti-war groups. The FBI sent a letter to Martin Luther King Jr. — timed to arrive just before King received the Nobel Peace Prize in 1964 — that called him an "evil, abnormal beast" and encouraged him to commit suicide. FBI agents provided the floor plan of Black Panther leader Fred Hampton's apartment to the Chicago police unit that raided it on December 4, 1969, killing Hampton in his bed. The program was exposed not by congressional oversight or journalistic investigation but by eight ordinary citizens who broke into an FBI field office in Media, Pennsylvania, in 1971 and mailed the stolen documents to newspapers.

Also not a theory. A documented, congressional-investigation-confirmed fact. And if you think that sounds like ancient history — something from a different era, before oversight committees and transparency laws cleaned everything up — I have bad news.

Operation Gladio. From the early 1950s through at least the 1990s, NATO and the CIA maintained secret "stay-behind" armies in virtually every Western European country — clandestine paramilitary networks designed to resist a potential Soviet invasion. Italian Prime Minister Giulio Andreotti confirmed their existence before the Italian Senate in 1990, and the European Parliament passed a resolution condemning the secret networks that same November. In Italy, some of these networks were linked to right-wing terrorist attacks — the so-called "strategy of tension" — including the 1980 Bologna railway station bombing that killed 85 people.

Operation Mockingbird. In 1977, Carl Bernstein — yes, the Watergate Carl Bernstein — published an investigation in Rolling Stone revealing that more than 400 American journalists had secretly carried out assignments for the CIA over the preceding twenty-five years. The Church Committee had confirmed that at least fifty journalists maintained covert CIA relationships. Among the publications involved: The New York Times, CBS, Time, Newsweek, and the Associated Press.

I could keep going. The Tuskegee syphilis experiment — in which the U.S. Public Health Service let 399 Black men go untreated for syphilis for forty years, from 1932 to 1972, just to study the disease's progression. Gulf of Tonkin — the "second attack" that Lyndon Johnson used to justify escalating the Vietnam War, which the NSA's own classified internal history later admitted almost certainly never happened. Iran-Contra — a scheme in which the Reagan administration secretly sold weapons to Iran and funneled the profits to right-wing rebels in Nicaragua, in direct violation of an act of Congress. The NSA mass surveillance program Edward Snowden exposed in 2013, which collected phone records and internet communications of millions of ordinary Americans who had done nothing wrong and were suspected of nothing. The list of times the United States government was caught doing exactly what it denied doing is not short. And it is not a list of paranoid fantasies.

It is a matter of public record.

---

So here we are, you and I, standing in a peculiar spot.

On one side: a documented history of governments lying to their citizens, running covert operations against domestic populations, dosing people with drugs, assassinating political leaders, and manipulating the press — all while publicly denying every accusation until the filing cabinets got pried open.

On the other side: an internet teeming with claims that shape-shifting lizard people run the banking system and that the COVID vaccine contains microchips linked to 5G towers.

Most people respond to this landscape by picking a team. Team One says everything is a conspiracy — every plane crash, every stock market dip, every election result is orchestrated by unseen hands. Team Two says conspiracies don't exist — that the phrase "conspiracy theory" is itself sufficient rebuttal, that anyone who questions official narratives is a crank who probably believes the moon landing was staged.

Both teams are wrong. Spectacularly, demonstrably, historically wrong.

The conspiracy-everywhere crowd makes a critical error: they assume that because some conspiracies were real, all conspiracy claims deserve equal weight. That's like arguing that because some people have been wrongly convicted of murder, nobody has ever actually committed one. It doesn't follow. The conspiracy-nowhere crowd makes the opposite error: they assume that because many conspiracy claims are ridiculous, the concept itself is illegitimate — a position that requires ignoring a filing cabinet's worth of declassified evidence and several decades of confirmed government misconduct.

And this book is for the people who refuse to join either one.

---

I should tell you where I stand. Not because my personal opinions are particularly interesting — they're not — but because intellectual honesty demands it, and because every author who claims to be "just presenting the facts" is usually smuggling in a worldview and hoping you won't notice.

I am not a conspiracy theorist. I do not believe that a secret cabal of elites meets in underground bunkers to determine the price of oil, rig elections, or engineer pandemics. I find the evidence for such claims remarkably thin — not merely unpersuasive, but often internally contradictory. The idea that thousands of people across dozens of countries could maintain a unified secret agenda for decades without a single credible whistleblower defies everything we know about human psychology, bureaucratic incompetence, and the difficulty of keeping even a surprise birthday party secret.

But I am not a naive institutionalist either. I do not believe that power operates transparently, that democratic processes function exactly as civics textbooks describe them, or that wealthy and influential people never coordinate their actions behind closed doors. That would require ignoring roughly half the twentieth century's declassified intelligence files.

What I am — what I've tried to be throughout the five years of research that produced this book — is an investigator. A fairly obsessive one. I've filed 147 Freedom of Information Act requests. I've read Senate committee transcripts that would put an insomniac to sleep. I've interviewed former intelligence officers, policy analysts, think-tank fellows, and three people who attended Bilderberg meetings (all of whom spoke on condition of anonymity, which tells you something). I've traveled to the Hotel De Bilderberg in Oosterbeek, the Netherlands — the actual hotel where the first meeting was held in 1954 — and found it now operates as a rather ordinary four-star property where you can book a room for €180 a night. I've walked the corridors of the Council on Foreign Relations headquarters in New York, attended a World Economic Forum regional meeting, and spent more hours than I care to admit in the National Archives reading documents with entire paragraphs blacked out by government censors.

What I found was neither as dramatic as conspiracy theorists claim nor as boring as skeptics insist. The truth — this is, frankly, almost always the case — sits in a gray zone that makes both sides uncomfortable. It's messier than a grand conspiracy. It's more troubling than a clean bill of health.

Some of the organizations examined in this book exercise genuine, documented influence over government policy. The evidence for this is extensive, specific, and comes from the organizations' own publications. The Council on Foreign Relations, for instance, has produced policy papers that became U.S. foreign policy almost verbatim — and this isn't a secret. They put it in their annual reports.

But "influence" is not the same as "control." And "coordination" is not the same as "conspiracy." The gap between those words — between what's documented and what's speculated — is where most people lose the thread. It's also where most books on this subject go off the rails, either inflating real influence into all-powerful puppet mastery or dismissing genuine institutional power as nothing worth worrying about.

I intend to do neither.

---

To help you — and, frankly, to help myself — sort through the claims you'll encounter in these pages, I've developed what I call the Conspiracy Evaluation Framework. Five questions. Simple to apply. Ruthlessly effective at separating what's documented from what's invented.

When confronted with any claim about hidden power, ask:

**One: Is there primary source documentation?** Declassified files, court records, FOIA documents, sworn testimony, leaked internal memos — the kind of evidence that would hold up in a courtroom. Not a YouTube video. Not someone's cousin's friend who "works in government." Paper. On the record.

**Two: Are there named individuals and specific dates?** Real conspiracies involve real people doing specific things at specific times. "The elites" is not a named individual. "March 15, 1973" is a specific date. If a claim can't provide names and dates, treat it with extreme suspicion.

**Three: Has it been confirmed by multiple independent sources?** MK-Ultra was confirmed by surviving CIA documents, congressional testimony, victim accounts, and journalistic investigations — all independently arriving at the same conclusions. If a claim relies on a single source, or on sources that all trace back to the same origin, the evidentiary foundation is shaky at best.

**Four: Has a government investigation acknowledged it?** Congressional committees, parliamentary inquiries, judicial proceedings, inspector general reports — when official bodies investigate and confirm a claim, the evidentiary bar has been cleared. This doesn't mean government denials prove something is false (governments denied MK-Ultra for twenty years). But official acknowledgment is a powerful data point.

**Five: Is there a falsifiable mechanism?** Can you explain *how* the alleged conspiracy works in concrete, logistically plausible terms? COINTELPRO had a clear mechanism: FBI agents infiltrated organizations, forged documents, and coordinated with local law enforcement. That's comprehensible. "A secret society controls the weather using space lasers" does not have a falsifiable mechanism. If a claim requires omniscient coordination, unlimited resources, and zero defections over decades, it is almost certainly wrong.

We'll apply this framework throughout the book — systematically, to every major claim. Some will pass all five tests. Most won't. And the ones that land somewhere in between — passing two or three tests, failing the rest — are the ones that deserve the most careful attention. Because the gray zone — the territory between "confirmed" and "debunked" — is where the real story lives. It's also where intellectual honesty gets hardest, because gray zones don't make for good social media posts or satisfying dinner-party arguments. They require you to hold two contradictory ideas in your head simultaneously: that power sometimes operates in secret, and that not every secret is a sign of omnipotent control.

# CHAPTER 1
## The Hotel That Became a Conspiracy

The lilacs were blooming early that year in Oosterbeek.

Józef Retinger noticed them from the garden terrace of the Hotel de Bilderberg — a converted Dutch country estate surrounded by beech trees and cycling paths, the kind of place where retired schoolteachers came for long weekends and American tourists never bothered to visit. It was May 29, 1954. Retinger was sixty-seven years old, five foot six, thin as a telephone pole, and so arthritic he needed help buttoning his shirt. He looked like an aging minor diplomat from a country that no longer existed in the form he remembered it.

He was not a minor anything.

From where he stood, smoking his third cigarette of the hour, he could see the black cars pulling up the gravel drive. A Rolls-Royce carried Prince Bernhard of the Netherlands, who would serve as chairman. Two dark sedans brought a pair of American senators whose names would have been front-page news in Washington. A Mercedes — the irony was not lost on Retinger — delivered a German industrialist who, four years earlier, had stood trial at Nuremberg. Behind them came the head of Unilever, the publisher of the *Washington Post*, a young politician from Britain named Denis Healey, and the president of the Rockefeller Foundation.

Sixty-seven men in total. No reporters. No aides allowed beyond the perimeter. No agenda distributed to anyone outside the hotel. The staff had been vetted by Dutch intelligence. The phone lines were monitored — not tapped, monitored, to ensure nobody called a newsroom.

Retinger watched the arrivals with the particular satisfaction of a man who had spent three years engineering exactly this moment. He'd written the invitation letters. He'd selected the participants. He'd convinced Allen Dulles — director of the Central Intelligence Agency — that American funding was essential. He'd persuaded Prince Bernhard that royal patronage would give the gathering legitimacy. And he'd chosen this hotel specifically because it was obscure enough that no journalist would think to look for sixty-seven of the most powerful men in the Western world in a small Dutch village best known for its role in the disastrous Operation Market Garden a decade earlier.

The meeting would last three days. It would produce no resolutions, no communiqué, no official record. The participants would return to their respective capitals and boardrooms and never publicly acknowledge what had been discussed.

And for the next fifty years, almost nobody outside the room would know it had happened at all.

---

To understand how a sickly Polish exile with no official title, no government position, and no personal fortune managed to create what would become the most infamous private gathering in modern history, you need to understand something about Józef Retinger that almost no one appreciated until long after his death in 1960: the man was, by any reasonable measure, the single best-connected human being in mid-twentieth-century Europe.

Born in Kraków in 1888, Retinger grew up in the fading twilight of the Austro-Hungarian Empire. His father was a lawyer with literary connections — close enough to the Polish intelligentsia that young Józef grew up around writers, politicians, and minor aristocrats who talked endlessly about Polish independence. By twenty, he spoke Polish, French, German, English, Italian, and Russian. By thirty, he had lived in London, Paris, and Mexico City, befriended Joseph Conrad (yes, the novelist — Retinger served as his personal secretary for a period), and cultivated relationships with an almost absurd range of powerful people across the political spectrum.

The list reads like someone made it up. Winston Churchill. Charles de Gaulle. The Belgian Prime Minister Paul-Henri Spaak. Allen Dulles, before and after he became CIA director. Averell Harriman, the American railroad heir turned diplomat. European royals from at least four countries. Senior Catholic clergy. Labor union leaders. Bankers. Generals. If you were important in Western Europe or North America between 1920 and 1960, there is a reasonable chance Józef Retinger had your phone number — and that you took his calls.

What made him extraordinary wasn't just the breadth of his network. Plenty of diplomats know powerful people. What set Retinger apart was his willingness to take physical risks that most diplomats would consider clinically insane.

In 1944, at age fifty-six — arthritic, fragile, and by his own admission terrified of heights — Retinger parachuted into Nazi-occupied Poland as part of a British Special Operations Executive mission. The drop went badly. His parachute barely opened, tangling in the lines, and he hit the ground hard enough to crack several ribs and temporarily paralyze his legs. He spent weeks hidden by the Polish resistance, unable to walk, conducting meetings with underground leaders from a cot in a basement in Warsaw. When he was finally extracted, the RAF pilot who flew the pickup later wrote that Retinger looked "like a man who had already died and simply forgotten to lie down."

He recovered. He always recovered. And by the time the war ended, Retinger had one big idea that consumed the final decade of his life.

Western civilization, he believed, was tearing itself apart. Europe was shattered. America was turning inward. The Soviet Union was expanding. And the people who could actually do something about it — the politicians, industrialists, bankers, and intellectuals who ran the Western world — never talked to each other candidly. They met at official summits where everything was scripted. They exchanged diplomatic cables that said nothing. They read each other's speeches and drew wrong conclusions.

What they needed, Retinger decided, was a private room. No cameras, no journalists, no public statements. A place where an American senator could tell a German industrialist what he actually thought about NATO, and the German could push back without worrying about a headline in *Der Spiegel* the next morning. A transatlantic salon for the powerful — invitation-only, off the record, and completely invisible to the public.

He pitched the concept to Prince Bernhard of the Netherlands in 1952. Bernhard — a German-born aristocrat who had married into the Dutch royal family, served in the Allied forces during the war, and carried himself with the kind of casual authority that only lifelong royals can manage — loved the idea. He had the social standing to attract participants. He had the political neutrality (the Netherlands was a small, non-threatening country) to host them. And he had, it must be said, a taste for intrigue that rivaled Retinger's own. (Bernhard would later be forced to resign from public life after accepting a $1.1 million bribe from Lockheed Martin — but that scandal was still two decades away.)

The Americans came on board through a more circuitous route. The CIA had already been funding transatlantic dialogue through a front organization called the American Committee for a United Europe, or ACUE. Declassified documents — released in the 1990s and meticulously catalogued by Georgetown University historian John Pomian-Srzednicki — show that ACUE channeled money to a range of European federalist movements during the early Cold War, part of Washington's strategy to build a unified Western Europe that could resist Soviet influence. Retinger's proposed gathering fit neatly into this framework. Dulles agreed to help fund it. So did the Ford Foundation and the Rockefeller Foundation, through channels that were never publicly disclosed at the time.

The invitation list for that first meeting in May 1954 was assembled by Retinger and a small steering committee over several months. The criteria were simple but exacting: attendees had to be influential in their respective countries, willing to speak frankly in private, and — this was non-negotiable — committed to keeping the proceedings confidential. Not secret, Retinger would later insist. Confidential. The distinction mattered to him, though it would matter less and less to the public as the decades rolled on.

---

So what, exactly, did those sixty-seven men discuss over three days in a Dutch country hotel while the lilacs bloomed outside?

The honest answer is: we don't know with certainty. No official minutes survive from the 1954 meeting — or if they do, they have never been released. What we have are reconstructions based on later interviews, memoirs, and a handful of documents that participants shared with trusted colleagues.

The Cold War dominated everything. In May 1954, the French were weeks away from their catastrophic defeat at Dien Bien Phu. Senator Joseph McCarthy was in the middle of his witch-hunt hearings in Washington. The Soviet Union had tested its first hydrogen bomb eight months earlier. And across Western Europe, communist parties were polling at 20-30% in France and Italy — numbers that genuinely terrified the Atlantic establishment.

The agenda, as best it can be reconstructed, focused on three questions. First: how to strengthen the Atlantic alliance at a moment when European anti-Americanism was rising and American isolationism was threatening a retreat. Second: how to manage the economic integration of Western Europe — the European Coal and Steel Community had been operating for two years, and the idea of a broader economic union was already circulating. Third: how to coordinate a Western response to Soviet expansionism without triggering a nuclear war.

These were not, to put it bluntly, the topics of a shadowy cabal plotting world domination. They were the standard anxieties of every Western foreign ministry and defense department in 1954. The difference was the format. At Bilderberg, a bank chairman could argue with a defense minister without diplomatic protocol. A newspaper publisher could challenge a senator's assumptions without the exchange appearing in tomorrow's paper. The Chatham House Rule — borrowed from the Royal Institute of International Affairs in London — applied: participants could later reference what was discussed, but never attribute a specific statement to a specific person.

In practice, even that mild standard of transparency collapsed. Nobody talked about Bilderberg publicly. The meetings happened; the participants went home; the world didn't notice. For years, the only published reference to the group appeared in a 1957 article in a minor Dutch magazine that attracted zero international attention.

---

The question you're probably asking — the one that separates the curious from the conspiratorial — is straightforward: does it matter?

Sixty-seven powerful men (and it was all men, for decades) sit in a room for three days. They talk. They don't vote on anything. They don't sign agreements. They go home. So what?

Here's what. The structure Retinger built in 1954 has survived, essentially unchanged, for seven decades. The annual meeting now draws roughly 130 participants from North America and Europe — occasionally expanding to include attendees from other regions. A steering committee of around thirty-five members selects participants and sets the agenda. Topics in recent years have included artificial intelligence regulation, the future of NATO, energy transition, China's geopolitical ambitions, and the state of democracy in the West. Attendees typically include sitting prime ministers, finance ministers, CEOs of multinational corporations, central bankers, senior military officials, prominent academics, and a handful of journalists (who attend under the same confidentiality rules as everyone else).

No votes are taken. No resolutions are passed. No binding decisions are made.

But here's the thing about a room full of the people who actually make binding decisions in their day jobs: they don't need to vote at Bilderberg. They vote at home. And when 130 people who collectively control a significant fraction of Western economic and military power spend three days talking about, say, the need for a common European currency — and then go home and pursue policies consistent with that conversation — the absence of a formal vote is a technicality.

Étienne Davignon understands this better than almost anyone alive. A Belgian businessman and politician who served as Bilderberg's chairman from 1999 to 2011, Davignon gave a remarkably candid interview to the BBC in 2009 in which he was asked directly about the group's influence on the creation of the euro. His answer was the closest thing to an admission of power that any Bilderberg insider has ever offered on the record.

"When we bentreated the euro, we had to agree on it," Davignon told the BBC's Bill Hayton. "The Euro could not have been achieved without the Bilderberg meetings."

Let that sit for a moment. The chairman of the most famously secretive elite gathering in the world, speaking on camera, told a reporter that a single currency adopted by nineteen sovereign nations — a currency that today underpins the financial lives of 340 million people — could not have happened without private discussions held behind closed doors, with no public record, no democratic mandate, and no accountability to any electorate.

Was Davignon exaggerating? Possibly. The history of European monetary integration is complex — it involved decades of negotiations, treaties, referenda, and political bargains that extended far beyond any single meeting or organization. The Maastricht Treaty of 1992, which established the legal framework for the euro, was the product of intergovernmental negotiations conducted in full public view (or at least as much public view as EU treaty-making ever gets). To say the euro "could not have happened" without Bilderberg is a strong claim.

But it's not an absurd one. Bilderberg meetings in the 1990s brought together exactly the people who would later drive monetary union — European commissioners, central bankers, finance ministers, and the CEOs of major banks who would have to manage the transition. The value of Bilderberg wasn't that it made the decision. The value was that it built the consensus that made the decision possible. By the time the formal negotiations began, the key players had already spent years in off-the-record conversations, testing arguments, identifying objections, and reaching informal understandings that greased the wheels of the official process.

This is the framework from the Introduction applied in real time. Ask the five questions.

*Is there documented evidence of coordination?* Yes — Davignon's own admission, corroborated by multiple attendees' memoirs and journalists' reconstructions published in outlets including the *Financial Times* and *The Guardian*.

*Is the mechanism of influence plausible?* Yes — private consensus-building among decision-makers is a well-understood precursor to policy action. You don't need a conspiracy when everyone already agrees.

*Is there a simpler explanation?* Partially. The people who wanted European monetary union would have pursued it regardless of Bilderberg. Many of the same conversations happened in other forums — the European Council, G7 summits, bilateral meetings. Bilderberg may have accelerated the process rather than created it.

*What is the scale of the claim?* Moderate. Nobody is claiming Bilderberg mind-controlled nineteen governments. The claim is that private elite dialogue smoothed the path for a policy outcome. That's influence, not omnipotence.

*Who benefits from the narrative?* Both sides. Bilderberg critics use the euro example to prove shadowy elites control the world. Bilderberg defenders use it to argue the group serves a useful function in international coordination. The truth, as usual, is less satisfying than either camp would prefer.

---

By the early 2000s, the Bilderberg Group had become something Józef Retinger never anticipated and almost certainly would have despised: famous.

The internet did what sixty years of journalism had failed to do — it made Bilderberg a household name, at least among the conspiracy-minded. Websites tracked the annual meeting locations. Amateur investigators staked out hotels, photographing license plates and cross-referencing guest lists. In 2006, Alex Jones — the InfoWars host who would later gain worldwide notoriety for claiming the Sandy Hook school shooting was staged — showed up outside the Bilderberg meeting in Ottawa with a megaphone, a camera crew, and an audience of millions who believed the attendees were plotting a New World Order.

The imagery was irresistible. Police cordons. Blacked-out SUVs. Men in suits disappearing behind security checkpoints. If you wanted to make the case that powerful people were hiding something, Bilderberg provided the perfect visual. And unlike most conspiracy theories, the basic facts were indisputable: these meetings were real, the attendees were genuinely powerful, and the proceedings genuinely were kept from the public. You didn't need to invent anything. The secrecy was right there, on camera, surrounded by police tape.

The group's response to this scrutiny was — and this is genuinely fascinating — to become more transparent. Slowly, grudgingly, and with the kind of institutional reluctance that large organizations specialize in, Bilderberg started publishing. Attendee lists appeared on an official website starting around 2010. Agenda topics — broad but real — were disclosed before each meeting. Press releases were issued. A media contact was designated.

The minutes, though? Still nothing. No transcripts. No summaries. No recordings. The Chatham House Rule, or rather Bilderberg's stricter interpretation of it, held firm. You could know who was in the room and what subjects were on the table. You could not know what anyone said about them.

This partial transparency created its own problems. For critics, the published attendee lists were confirmation of everything they'd suspected — look at these names, they'd say, pointing to Kissinger, to Schmidt, to Thiel, to Gates. *These people are deciding your future and you didn't elect any of them.* For defenders, the transparency was evidence of good faith — *we're telling you who's here and what we're discussing; what more do you want?*

The answer, of course, was: the minutes. The actual content of the discussions. The words that were spoken. And on that point, Bilderberg's position has not moved an inch since 1954.

You've probably noticed something about this chapter, and it's worth naming explicitly: I haven't told you whether Bilderberg is good or bad.

That's intentional. The point of this book is not to hand you a verdict. The point is to give you the tools to reach your own. And the Bilderberg Group — precisely because it sits so squarely in the gray zone between legitimate private dialogue and unaccountable elite coordination — is the perfect place to start.

Here's what the evidence supports. Bilderberg is real. Its participants are genuinely powerful. Its discussions are genuinely private. And in at least one major case — the creation of the euro — its own leadership has acknowledged that the group played a meaningful role in shaping a policy outcome that affected hundreds of millions of people who had no voice in the conversation.

Here's what the evidence does not support. Bilderberg does not run the world. It does not issue orders to governments. It does not have a master plan, a secret charter, or a shadowy enforcement mechanism. It is not, as some of the more heated corners of the internet insist, a satanic cabal or a precursor to a one-world government. The gap between "influential private forum" and "all-powerful shadow government" is enormous, and the people who collapse that gap are doing so without evidence — and usually with a book to sell or an audience to feed.

The real question isn't whether Bilderberg has power. Of course it does — any gathering of people with that much collective authority has power by definition. The real question is whether that kind of private, unaccountable power is compatible with democracy. Whether elected leaders should be having off-the-record conversations with unelected billionaires about policies that will affect billions of people. Whether the Chatham House Rule, designed for a 1954 world of gentlemen's agreements and Cold War urgency, makes any sense in an era of WikiLeaks, citizen journalism, and institutional distrust running at historic highs.

Those are questions worth wrestling with. They don't require a conspiracy theory. They require a democracy.

But Bilderberg was never the only game in town — and not even the oldest. Three decades before Retinger's lilacs bloomed in Oosterbeek, a group of American scholars sat down in a Paris hotel room with Woodrow Wilson and drafted a blueprint for an institution that would shape U.S. foreign policy for the next century. They called it the Council on Foreign Relations. And unlike Bilderberg, the Council didn't bother hiding. It put its address on Park Avenue, published a journal called *Foreign Affairs*, and embedded itself so deeply in the American establishment that by the time anyone thought to ask whether this was appropriate, it was already too late to disentangle.

That's Chapter 2.

# CHAPTER 2
## One Hundred and Eight Bankers Walk Into a Room

Between 1940 and 2001, fourteen individuals served as United States Secretary of State. Thirteen were members of the Council on Foreign Relations.

During the same period, every single National Security Advisor — from McGeorge Bundy under Kennedy to Condoleezza Rice under George W. Bush — held CFR membership. Eight CIA directors. Seven Treasury Secretaries. Multiple World Bank presidents, NATO commanders, and Federal Reserve chairs. The list goes on. And on. And on.

If you're an organization with roughly 5,000 members in a nation of 330 million, and your membership consistently occupies the most sensitive posts in the national security apparatus across both Democratic and Republican administrations for six consecutive decades, one of two things is true. Either you're running the world's most successful conspiracy. Or you're running the world's most successful professional network.

The answer — unsatisfying as it may be — is closer to the second. But "closer to" is doing a lot of heavy lifting in that sentence, and the distance between a professional network and an unelected shadow foreign ministry is shorter than most people would like to admit.

To understand how we got here, you need to go back to 1919, to a ruined continent and a hotel room full of men who believed they could fix the world if everyone else would just get out of the way.

---

Elihu Root was seventy-four years old in 1919, and he had already lived more lives than most statesmen are granted.

Secretary of War under McKinley. Secretary of State under Theodore Roosevelt. United States Senator from New York. Winner of the Nobel Peace Prize in 1912 — for his work establishing the Central American Court of Justice, among other diplomatic achievements that sound impossibly quaint a century later. Root was the kind of man whose résumé makes you suspicious that he's fictional, a composite character invented for a textbook.

He wasn't. He was very real, very sharp, and very worried.

Root's worry — the one that consumed his final decades — was elegant in its simplicity and terrifying in its implications. He believed that democracy had a fatal design flaw. Not corruption. Not inequality. Not authoritarianism creeping in from the edges. The flaw, as Root saw it, was *ignorance*. Specifically, the ignorance of the American voter about anything happening beyond the country's borders.

"The control of foreign relations by modern democracies creates a new and pressing demand for popular education in international affairs," Root wrote in 1922. The phrasing is polite. The subtext is less so. What Root meant — what he said more bluntly in private — was that the average American citizen knew almost nothing about how the world worked, cared even less, and was therefore catastrophically susceptible to emotional manipulation by demagogues who understood that "foreigners are bad" was always an easier sell than "here's why the Treaty of Versailles matters for grain prices in Kansas."

Root wasn't wrong about the problem. His solution, though, contained seeds that are still sprouting today.

---

Paris, 1919. The war to end all wars had ended, and the diplomats had arrived to divide the spoils.

At the Hôtel Majestic on Avenue Kléber, a group of British and American foreign policy specialists gathered for a series of dinners that had nothing to do with the formal peace negotiations and everything to do with what would come after. The British delegation included Lionel Curtis, a Round Table Movement organizer who believed the English-speaking peoples had a civilizing mission that required institutional backing. The Americans included a younger cohort of scholars and diplomats — men from Yale, Harvard, Princeton — who had served as advisors to Woodrow Wilson's "Inquiry," the secret study group that had prepared America's negotiating positions for the conference.

Their idea was straightforward: create a permanent Anglo-American institute for the study of international affairs. One branch in London. One branch in New York. Shared research, shared conferences, shared assumptions about the proper ordering of the world.

The British went home and founded the Royal Institute of International Affairs — Chatham House, which we met in the previous chapter. The Americans went home and... did nothing. For two years.

The grand Anglo-American institute never materialized. What happened instead was smaller, more American, and in the long run, more powerful. In 1921, the American group merged with an existing New York dinner club — a collection of bankers, lawyers, and financiers who had been meeting informally since 1918 to discuss international commerce — and incorporated as the Council on Foreign Relations.

One hundred and eight founding members. The roster reads like a casting call for a period drama about the American establishment: partners from J.P. Morgan, lawyers from Sullivan & Cromwell, professors from Columbia and Harvard, a handful of journalists who believed their job was to inform the elite rather than antagonize it. Root, by then the most distinguished elder statesman alive, lent his name and his prestige. He didn't run the organization — that fell to younger men — but his philosophy shaped its DNA.

The philosophy was this: American foreign policy was too important to be left to Congress, too complex to be left to the public, and too consequential to be left to the State Department alone. What was needed was a space — private but not secret, exclusive but not closed — where the people who actually made foreign policy decisions could think out loud without worrying about tomorrow's headlines.

They found that space on East 68th Street.

---

Harold Pratt House is a five-story Italianate mansion built in 1920 for Harold Irving Pratt, a Standard Oil executive and son of Charles Pratt, one of John D. Rockefeller's original business partners. When Pratt died in 1939, his widow donated the house to the Council on Foreign Relations. The CFR moved in during 1945 and has never left.

Walk past it today — 58 East 68th Street, on the Upper East Side, between Park and Madison — and you'll see a building that looks exactly like what it is: old money, understated, unapologetic. No flashy signage. No security theater. Just a brass plaque, a doorman, and the quiet confidence of an institution that has never needed to advertise.

Inside Pratt House, the CFR built what became — without exaggeration — the most influential private foreign policy operation in American history. And they did it largely through two mechanisms: a journal and a revolving door.

The journal came first. *Foreign Affairs*, launched in 1922, became the publication where American foreign policy was debated, tested, and occasionally invented. George Kennan's "X Article" — "The Sources of Soviet Conduct," published in *Foreign Affairs* in July 1947 under the pseudonym "X" — laid out the containment doctrine that would define America's Cold War strategy for four decades. Kennan wasn't just a State Department official publishing an essay. He was a CFR member, operating within CFR intellectual circles, whose ideas had been refined through CFR study groups before they ever reached print.

That's the pattern. Not conspiracy. *Incubation.*

Samuel Huntington's "The Clash of Civilizations?" appeared in *Foreign Affairs* in 1993 — the essay that reframed post–Cold War geopolitics around cultural and religious fault lines. Whether you agree with Huntington or find him reductive, his framework shaped twenty years of American foreign policy debates. It started as a CFR paper.

The revolving door was the second mechanism, and it worked with a precision that even its critics have to admire. Here's how it functioned in practice. A young foreign policy scholar joins the CFR as a term member — a five-year membership for promising professionals under forty. She attends study groups, writes policy papers, builds relationships with senior members who hold (or recently held) government positions. A Democratic administration takes office. The new Secretary of State, himself a CFR member, needs staffers who understand the Atlantic alliance. He calls people he knows. People from the study groups. People from the dinners at Pratt House.

Our scholar goes to Washington. Serves four or eight years. Leaves government. Returns to the CFR — now as a senior fellow, lending her government experience to the next generation of term members who will, in turn, go to Washington when their party wins. The cycle repeats. Election after election. Decade after decade.

This is how Dean Rusk went from CFR president to Secretary of State under Kennedy and Johnson. This is how Cyrus Vance, a CFR director, became Carter's Secretary of State. This is how Dick Cheney, Donald Rumsfeld, and Colin Powell — all CFR members — occupied three of the four most powerful national security positions in the George W. Bush administration, despite Bush himself running on a platform that was skeptical of the Eastern establishment that the CFR embodied.

The revolving door didn't care about party affiliation. It cared about *access*.

---

If there's a single case study that captures the CFR's actual power — not imagined, not inflated, but real and documented — it's the War and Peace Studies project.

September 1939. Germany invades Poland. The Second World War begins. And within weeks, the CFR approaches the State Department with a proposal: let us study the postwar world. Not as government employees. Not on the government payroll. As private citizens, funded by the Rockefeller Foundation, doing the long-term strategic thinking that the State Department — overwhelmed by the daily crises of wartime diplomacy — simply didn't have the bandwidth to do.

The State Department said yes.

For six years, from 1939 to 1945, the War and Peace Studies project produced 682 meetings and 362 memoranda, all delivered directly to the State Department and, in many cases, to President Roosevelt's inner circle. The project was divided into four groups: Economic and Financial, Security and Armaments, Territorial, and Political. The participants included some of the sharpest minds in American academia and finance. The funding — approximately $350,000 over six years, serious money in 1940s dollars — came entirely from the Rockefeller Foundation (Council on Foreign Relations, "War and Peace Studies," CFR Historical Archives).

What did these memoranda contain? Blueprints. For the United Nations. For the International Monetary Fund. For the World Bank. For the reconstruction of Europe and Japan. For the architecture of the postwar international order that — love it or hate it — has governed global affairs for eighty years.

The Marshall Plan, announced by Secretary of State George Marshall in June 1947, is routinely credited to Marshall himself, to George Kennan's Policy Planning Staff, and to Undersecretary of State Dean Acheson. All true. But the intellectual architecture — the idea that America's strategic interest required the economic reconstruction of former enemies, that aid should be conditional on European cooperation, that the goal was markets rather than colonies — had been worked out in CFR study groups years before Marshall gave his Harvard commencement speech.

Now. Stop for a moment and apply the five-question framework we established in Chapter 1.

Was this secret? Not exactly. The War and Peace Studies project wasn't publicized, but it wasn't hidden either. The State Department knew. The Rockefeller Foundation's grants were documented. Participants were respected scholars and professionals, not shadowy operatives.

Was it democratic? Absolutely not. No one elected these people. No congressional committee authorized the project. No voter had any input into the memoranda that shaped postwar planning. 362 documents influencing the reconstruction of the world, produced by a private club in Manhattan funded by one of the richest families in human history.

Was the outcome harmful? That depends on your view of the postwar order itself. The Marshall Plan rebuilt Europe, prevented mass starvation, and arguably stopped Western Europe from going communist. The UN, the IMF, the World Bank — these institutions have been credited with preventing a third world war, stabilizing global commerce, and lifting billions out of poverty. They've also been accused of perpetuating American hegemony, enabling financial exploitation of developing nations, and imposing Western values on cultures that didn't ask for them. Pick your lens.

Was it a conspiracy? No. It was a think tank doing what think tanks do — producing research and hoping decision-makers would read it. The difference between the CFR and, say, a random policy shop in Washington is that the CFR's members *were* the decision-makers. The people writing the memoranda and the people receiving them often attended the same dinners, belonged to the same clubs, and had gone to the same schools.

Is that a problem? You tell me.

The critics arrived right on schedule.

In the 1960s and 1970s, the John Birch Society — a far-right organization founded by candy manufacturer Robert Welch in 1958 — made the CFR a central villain in its theory of a communist conspiracy to create a one-world government. The Birchers distributed pamphlets, organized lectures, and published newsletters naming CFR members as traitors. Their logic was circular but effective: the CFR is full of powerful people; powerful people make bad things happen; bad things are happening; therefore the CFR is responsible. QED.

Phyllis Schlafly picked up the theme in 1964 with *A Choice Not an Echo*, a self-published polemic that sold over three million copies. Schlafly argued that a small group of Eastern establishment kingmakers — centered on the CFR — had controlled Republican presidential nominations since 1936, ensuring that only candidates sympathetic to internationalism (and, by her implication, soft on communism) received the party's backing. Goldwater's nomination that year was supposed to break the pattern. It did. He lost forty-four states.

Pat Robertson went further. Much further. In *The New World Order*, published in 1991 — three years before he ran for president — Robertson wove the CFR into a conspiracy theory that included the Illuminati, the Freemasons, European banking families, and explicitly anti-Semitic tropes about a centuries-old plot to establish a satanic world government. The book sold over 500,000 copies. Robertson was not a fringe figure. He was a televangelist with a daily audience of millions, a former presidential candidate, and the founder of the Christian Coalition, one of the most powerful political organizations in late-twentieth-century America.

The irony — and it is a thick, almost suffocating irony — is that the CFR was, during this entire period, one of the most transparent think tanks in Washington. *Foreign Affairs* was (and is) available at any library. CFR reports were published and distributed freely. The membership list, while not posted on a billboard, was never a closely guarded secret. Starting in the early 2000s, cfr.org became one of the most comprehensive foreign policy resources on the internet, offering thousands of free articles, backgrounders, and policy analyses to anyone with a browser.

Secret cabals don't typically publish quarterly journals, maintain extensive websites, host on-the-record public events, and invite C-SPAN cameras into their conference rooms. The CFR did all of these things, routinely, for decades. And it made absolutely no difference to the conspiracy theorists, because the conspiracy was never really about the CFR's behavior. It was about the *feeling* — the gut-level, visceral, entirely understandable feeling — that somewhere, somehow, a small group of people you didn't elect were making decisions that shaped your life without your knowledge or consent.

That feeling wasn't wrong. The conclusions drawn from it often were.

Here's what you should take away from the Council on Foreign Relations, and it's a framework you can apply to every institution in this book.

Ask yourself five questions about any organization accused of running the world:

First — *who's in the room?* The CFR's 5,000 members include former presidents, CEOs, journalists, academics, and military leaders. The membership skews elite, Northeastern, Ivy League–educated, and (historically) overwhelmingly white and male. This matters not because elites are evil, but because homogeneous groups produce homogeneous thinking, and homogeneous thinking applied to global policy tends to serve the people in the room better than the people outside it.

Second — *what's the pipeline?* Follow the career paths. CFR member → government appointee → CFR fellow → government appointee. The pipeline is real, documented, and self-reinforcing. It doesn't require a conspiracy to function. It requires only that powerful people prefer to hire other powerful people they already know — which is how every industry in human history has operated.

Third — *what's the output?* Read the CFR's actual publications. They're public. They're on the website. Most of them are sober, wonkish, and about as exciting as a tax code revision. If you're expecting a secret plan for world domination, you'll be disappointed. If you're looking for evidence that a narrow intellectual class shapes the boundaries of acceptable foreign policy debate in America, you'll find plenty.

Fourth — *who funds it?* The Rockefeller family. The Carnegie Corporation. The Ford Foundation. Corporate members including Goldman Sachs, ExxonMobil, and JPMorgan Chase. This isn't secret — it's on the CFR's annual report. But it matters. The people who fund the research shape the questions that get asked, and the questions that get asked shape the answers that reach the president's desk.

Fifth — *what's missing from the room?* No labor organizers. No community activists. No representatives from the Global South, at least not until very recently. The CFR's vision of the world has always been a view from the top floor of a Manhattan skyscraper — panoramic, confident, and fundamentally blind to what the street looks like from ground level.

None of this makes the CFR a conspiracy. All of it makes the CFR a problem — the kind of structural, systemic, deeply embedded problem that democracies have never figured out how to solve. How do you make foreign policy without experts? You can't. How do you prevent the experts from becoming a self-perpetuating class with their own interests and blind spots? Nobody knows.

But by 1973, the world was shifting under the CFR's feet.

Europe was no longer a bombed-out continent in need of American rescue plans. Japan had transformed from occupied nation to economic juggernaut. The oil crisis was about to scramble every assumption about global power. And David Rockefeller — grandson of John D., chairman of Chase Manhattan Bank, and the closest thing the American establishment had to a living mascot — was growing restless.

Rockefeller looked at the CFR and saw something too narrow. Too Atlantic. Too locked into the Cold War binary of East versus West when the real action was shifting to a triangular world: North America, Western Europe, and a rising Asia that the old institutions weren't equipped to handle.

He found a kindred spirit in Zbigniew Brzezinski, a Columbia University political scientist with a Polish accent, a talent for grand strategy, and an appetite for institution-building that matched Rockefeller's own. Brzezinski had published *Between Two Ages* in 1970, arguing that the world was entering a "technetronic era" that required new forms of international cooperation beyond the Atlantic alliance.

Together, Rockefeller and Brzezinski did what men of their particular temperament always do when they see a gap in the institutional landscape. They filled it. In July 1973, they convened a meeting in Tokyo — bankers, politicians, academics from North America, Europe, and Japan — and announced the formation of a new organization.

They called it the Trilateral Commission. And with it, the modern conspiracy movement found the villain it had been waiting for.

# CHAPTER 3
## David Rockefeller's Rolodex

When David Rockefeller died on March 20, 2017 — a Monday morning, at his estate in Pocantico Hills, New York, age 101 — the obituaries ran exactly the way you'd expect. Philanthropist. Banker. Statesman. Grandson of the world's first billionaire. Chairman emeritus of the Council on Foreign Relations. Founder of the Trilateral Commission. Honorary everything. The *New York Times* gave him four full pages.

But the detail that stuck — the one that burrowed into public consciousness and stayed there — wasn't about banks or foundations or presidents. It was about a Rolodex.

David Rockefeller maintained a personal Rolodex containing more than 150,000 entries. Not a database. Not a CRM system. A physical, hand-maintained Rolodex — the old-fashioned rotating card file — that he updated personally, every single night, for over four decades. Every person he met at a dinner party. Every ambassador who shook his hand at Davos. Every phone call, every handwritten thank-you note, every birthday remembered. Catalogued, cross-referenced, annotated in his own handwriting. When the Rockefeller Archive Center in Sleepy Hollow eventually took possession of the collection, it filled an entire room.

Think about that for a second.

This was not a man who left relationships to chance. This was a man who treated the cultivation of human connections the way his grandfather treated oil wells — systematically, relentlessly, and with an understanding that the real asset wasn't any single relationship but the *network itself*. One hundred fifty thousand names. The entire Western power structure, filed alphabetically and updated before bed like a diary.

And it's this instinct — this compulsive, almost pathological need to connect, organize, and orchestrate — that explains everything that followed. The Trilateral Commission didn't emerge from ideology. It emerged from a Rolodex. From a man who believed, down to his marrow, that the right people in the right room could solve any problem on earth.

He might have been right. He might have been dangerously wrong. But either way, he built the machine.

---

To understand David Rockefeller, you have to understand the weight of the name he carried.

His grandfather, John D. Rockefeller, didn't just dominate the oil industry — he *invented* the concept of industrial monopoly. Standard Oil, at its peak in the 1890s, controlled roughly 91% of oil refining and 85% of oil sales in the United States, according to historian Ron Chernow's definitive biography *Titan* (1998). The Supreme Court broke up Standard Oil in 1911. The family fortune survived intact. By some estimates, John D. Rockefeller's inflation-adjusted net worth would exceed $400 billion today — making him the wealthiest American who ever lived, by a comfortable margin.

David was the youngest of John D. Rockefeller Jr.'s six children. Born June 12, 1915, in a world that already bore his family's name on universities, hospitals, museums, and an entire section of midtown Manhattan. He earned a bachelor's degree from Harvard in 1936, studied at the London School of Economics (where he briefly encountered the young John F. Kennedy), and completed his PhD in economics at the University of Chicago in 1940 — writing his dissertation on Fabian socialism, of all things, under the supervision of Joseph Schumpeter.

That last detail matters more than it seems. Rockefeller didn't just inherit wealth. He studied how economic systems worked, how they broke down, and how power moved through institutions. His academic training was rigorous, cosmopolitan, and deeply informed by the failures of the interwar period — the collapse of international cooperation, the rise of nationalism, the catastrophic consequences of economic isolation. Everything he built later grew from this soil.

After wartime military intelligence work in North Africa and France, Rockefeller joined Chase National Bank in 1946. By 1969, he was CEO of Chase Manhattan (the merged entity), a position he held until 1981. During those twelve years, he transformed Chase from a domestic bank into one of the first truly global financial institutions, opening branches in Moscow, Beijing, Cairo — places American bankers didn't go during the Cold War. He met with Khrushchev. He met with Mao. He met with Sadat, with the Shah, with virtually every head of state in the Western and non-Western world.

And every single one of them went into the Rolodex.

Here's the thing about Rockefeller that the conspiracy theories get exactly backward. The conspiracists paint him as a shadowy puppet master, pulling strings from behind a curtain. The reality was the opposite — Rockefeller operated in plain sight, almost ostentatiously. He *wanted* people to know he was connecting governments, corporations, and institutions across borders. He considered it his life's mission. His 2002 memoir, simply titled *Memoirs*, contains a passage on page 405 that became the single most-cited piece of evidence in the entire conspiracy literature:

> "Some even believe we are part of a secret cabal working against the best interests of the United States, characterizing my family and me as 'internationalists' and of conspiring with others around the world to build a more integrated global political and economic structure — one world, if you will. If that's the charge, I stand guilty, and I am proud of it."

Conspiracy theorists read that quote and hear a confession. *See? He admitted it!*

But read it again, carefully. What Rockefeller is actually saying is: *Yes, I believe in international cooperation, and I don't think that's something to be ashamed of.* He's not confessing to a secret plot. He's refusing to apologize for his worldview. There's a difference — though the conspiracy literature has never been particularly interested in that distinction.

---

July 1973. Tokyo.

The world was coming apart at the seams, or at least rearranging itself in ways that made the old institutional architecture look obsolete. Nixon had yanked the dollar off the gold standard two years earlier — the so-called "Nixon Shock" — and the global monetary system was still reeling. The Yom Kippur War was months away, and with it the OPEC oil embargo that would quadruple energy prices overnight and send the Western economies into stagflation. Japan, meanwhile, had gone from postwar rubble to the world's second-largest economy in barely two decades, and nobody in the Atlantic-focused foreign policy establishment seemed to know what to do about it.

Rockefeller saw the gap. So did Zbigniew Brzezinski.

Brzezinski — born in Warsaw, educated at McGill and Harvard, a professor at Columbia with the kind of strategic mind that made Henry Kissinger nervous — had been arguing since the late 1960s that the old bipolar Cold War framework was crumbling. His 1970 book *Between Two Ages: America's Role in the Technetronic Era* laid out a vision of a world where technology, communication, and economics were dissolving national boundaries faster than diplomats could draw them. The CFR was fine for managing the Atlantic relationship, Brzezinski argued. But the Atlantic relationship wasn't where the future lived.

Rockefeller agreed. And when David Rockefeller agreed with your thesis, things happened fast.

In the summer of 1973, they convened approximately 180 leaders from North America, Western Europe, and Japan at the first meeting of the Trilateral Commission. The name was the point — three sides of a triangle, three pillars of the industrialized democratic world, three regions that needed to coordinate economic and political strategy if the liberal international order was going to survive the shocks hitting it from every direction.

The structure was straightforward. Three regional groups — North American, European, and Asia-Pacific — each with roughly 100 to 130 members drawn from business, government (mostly former officials), academia, and media. A total membership hovering around 390 people, give or take, depending on the year. Each regional group had its own chairman. The three groups met separately throughout the year and convened together for one annual plenary meeting. An executive committee set the agenda. A small secretariat — initially based in New York, with satellite offices in Paris and Tokyo — handled logistics.

The budget? Modest. A few million dollars annually, funded by member contributions, corporate sponsorships, and foundation grants. For an organization supposedly running the world, the Trilateral Commission operated with roughly the budget of a mid-tier regional nonprofit.

And here's the part that should make every conspiracy theorist pause: the Trilateral Commission published everything. All of it. Their policy reports — called "Triangle Papers" — were printed, distributed, and made publicly available. You could (and still can) read them. They covered trade policy, energy security, monetary coordination, democratic governance, nuclear proliferation. The prose style ran toward the aggressively wonkish. Dense paragraphs about tariff structures and currency stabilization mechanisms. Not exactly the stuff of shadowy world domination.

If these people were plotting to install a new world order, they were doing it in the most boring way imaginable — through position papers on agricultural trade liberalization and joint communiqués about the importance of macroeconomic coordination. The gap between the conspiracy mythology and the actual documentary record is almost comically large.

Almost.

Because then there was Jimmy Carter.

And the Carter story is where things get genuinely interesting — not because the conspiracy theory is right, but because the *facts themselves* are strange enough to make reasonable people uncomfortable.

Here's the timeline, stripped to its bones:

1971: James Earl Carter Jr. is the governor of Georgia. A peanut farmer turned state politician. Virtually unknown outside the South. Nobody — and I mean *nobody* — in Washington considers him a serious contender for national office.

1973: Brzezinski is recruiting members for the newly formed Trilateral Commission. He's looking for promising young political figures who represent the future of American leadership — people outside the Northeastern establishment. Someone recommends Carter. Brzezinski invites him. Carter accepts.

1974: Carter attends Trilateral Commission meetings. He meets Rockefeller, Brzezinski, and dozens of the most connected people in American foreign policy and finance. For a governor from Plains, Georgia (population: 683), this is like being handed a backstage pass to the entire power structure.

1975: Carter announces his candidacy for President of the United States. The national press corps treats it as a joke. *Jimmy who?*

1976: Carter wins the Democratic nomination. Then the presidency. The peanut farmer from nowhere is the leader of the free world.

1977: Carter takes office and appoints his cabinet. Twenty-six members of the Trilateral Commission receive positions in the new administration — including Brzezinski himself as National Security Advisor, Cyrus Vance as Secretary of State, W. Michael Blumenthal as Secretary of the Treasury, and Harold Brown as Secretary of Defense. The Trilateral Commission, an organization barely four years old, had placed its members in the four most powerful national security positions in the United States government.

Twenty-six appointments. From a membership pool of roughly 300 Americans. That's not a coincidence. That's a batting average that would make a baseball scout weep.

The conspiracy interpretation writes itself, and it was written — loudly, repeatedly, and with increasing hysteria throughout the late 1970s. Senator Barry Goldwater, in his 1979 book *With No Apologies*, called the Trilateral Commission "David Rockefeller's newest international cabal" and accused it of grooming Carter for the presidency. Lyndon LaRouche, the perennial fringe candidate, made "Trilateral Commission controls the White House" a centerpiece of his political campaigns. Evangelical broadcaster Pat Robertson, in his 1991 book *The New World Order*, wove the Commission into a vast conspiratorial tapestry stretching back centuries.

The machinery of suspicion churned. And the raw material it was working with — those twenty-six appointments, that meteoric rise from obscurity — was, on its face, genuinely remarkable.

So what actually happened?

Apply the five-question framework from the previous chapter — the same lens we used on the CFR — and the picture comes into focus. Not as a conspiracy. Not as an innocent coincidence. As something in between, and arguably more troubling than either.

*Who was in the room?* Approximately 390 members, drawn from the senior ranks of business, government, academia, and media across three continents. Not random citizens. Not a cross-section of society. The elite of the elite, self-selected and invitation-only.

*What was the pipeline?* Trilateral Commission membership → access to the most powerful network in the industrialized world → credibility and connections that translate directly into political viability → government appointment. The pipeline didn't require smoke-filled rooms or secret handshakes. It required only that membership in the Commission functioned as a credential — a signal to donors, party officials, and media gatekeepers that this person was "serious." And it did.

Carter didn't become president *because* the Trilateral Commission installed him. He became president because Trilateral membership gave an unknown governor access to a network, a worldview, and a donor base that would have taken decades to build on his own. The Commission didn't pick the president. The Commission *accelerated* a career. There's a meaningful difference, though the end result — twenty-six Commission members in the administration — looked identical from the outside.

*What was the output?* Triangle Papers on trade, energy, defense, and monetary policy. Publicly available. Often tedious. Consistently advocating for deeper economic integration between the three regions, reduced trade barriers, and coordinated responses to global crises. In other words: exactly the policy positions you'd expect from an organization founded by a global banker and a professor of international relations. The output wasn't secret. It was predictable.

*Who funded it?* Member dues, corporate sponsors, and foundations — many of them Rockefeller-affiliated. The budget ran to a few million dollars a year. Not nothing, but hardly the financial footprint of a world-dominating conspiracy. The Trilateral Commission spent less annually than a single U.S. congressional campaign.

*What was missing from the room?* Same thing that was missing from the CFR, amplified by geography. No labor leaders. No representatives of developing nations (the Commission was explicitly limited to the "trilateral" world — North America, Europe, Japan — until expanding to include South Korea, Australia, and other Asia-Pacific nations in later decades). No voices from the Global South, where the economic policies being discussed in those Triangle Papers would have their most dramatic effects. The Trilateral Commission's vision of global cooperation was cooperation among the already powerful — a club of winners designing rules that, coincidentally, tended to benefit winners.

Here's the uncomfortable truth that neither the conspiracy theorists nor the Commission's defenders want to acknowledge.

The Trilateral Commission wasn't a secret government. It was something more subtle and, in some ways, more corrosive: a *parallel* credentialing system. A mechanism by which a small group of private citizens — unelected, unaccountable, operating outside any democratic framework — could effectively pre-screen candidates for the highest levels of government. Not by rigging elections. Not by stuffing ballot boxes. By doing something far more elegant: building a network so powerful that membership in it became a prerequisite for being taken seriously.

You didn't need a conspiracy to explain Jimmy Carter's twenty-six Trilateral appointments. You needed only to understand that when a president — any president — fills a cabinet, he draws from the people he knows. And the people Carter knew, by 1976, were disproportionately Trilateral Commission members. The conspiracy was hiding in plain sight, except it wasn't a conspiracy at all. It was networking. The most effective networking operation in the history of American politics.

And David Rockefeller — the man with the 150,000-name Rolodex, the man who believed that the right connections could solve any problem — had designed it that way from the start. Not out of malice. Out of conviction. He genuinely believed that the people in his Rolodex were the best people to run the world. He wasn't trying to undermine democracy. He simply didn't think democracy, left to its own devices, would produce leaders sophisticated enough to manage an increasingly complex global system.

Whether that makes him a visionary or a threat to self-governance depends entirely on your faith in ordinary people to choose wisely. Rockefeller's faith, to put it mildly, was limited.

The pattern didn't end with Carter. Ronald Reagan's administration, despite its populist, anti-establishment rhetoric, included at least sixteen Trilateral Commission members and associates in senior positions, according to researcher Laurence Shoup's analysis in *Wall Street's Think Tank* (2019). George H.W. Bush — a former Trilateral member himself — continued the tradition. Bill Clinton attended Bilderberg in 1991, the year before his own dark-horse presidential run. The faces changed. The network didn't.

And the Trilateral Commission, for its part, adapted. It expanded beyond its original trilateral framework — adding members from South Korea, Australia, New Zealand, and eventually broadening the Asia-Pacific group to include several ASEAN nations. By the 2020s, membership had crept past 400. The Triangle Papers kept coming. The annual plenaries kept convening. The organization that critics called a puppet master kept doing what it had always done: bringing the same kinds of people into the same kinds of rooms to discuss the same kinds of problems, then publishing the results for anyone who cared to read them.

Almost nobody did. Which might be the most damning detail of all — not that the Trilateral Commission operates in secret, but that it operates in a kind of performative transparency that functions identically to secrecy. Publishing a 90-page policy paper on multilateral trade coordination is, functionally, the same as classifying it. Nobody outside the membership reads it. The conspiracy theorists are too busy looking for hidden codes. The general public has never heard of it. And the members — the 400 people who actually matter — already discussed it over dinner at the plenary.

Transparency without audience is indistinguishable from secrecy. Remember that. It'll come up again.
