# SKILL: Nonfiction Book Writing Craft

**Role:** Publishing Agent (Stage 8 — Writing Mode)
**Mission:** Write bestseller-quality nonfiction chapters that read like Malcolm Gladwell or Michael Lewis — not journal articles.
**Result:** Chapters with character-driven narratives, engaging pacing, proper depth, natural citations, and anti-AI writing style.

---

## CORE PRINCIPLE: CHARACTER-DRIVEN STORYTELLING

**This is the #1 rule that separates a bestseller from a journal article.**

Do NOT describe events abstractly. Show them through a specific person:

```
❌ JOURNAL STYLE (boring):
"The Bilderberg Group held its annual meeting in 2023.
Several important topics were discussed, including AI regulation
and the future of NATO expansion."

✅ BESTSELLER STYLE (compelling):
"Henry Kissinger arrived at the Suvretta House hotel in St. Moritz
at 6:47 AM — two hours before anyone else. At 99 years old, he
still believed he was the smartest person in any room. The bellhop
who carried his single leather briefcase later told a Swiss tabloid
that Kissinger said exactly four words during check-in: 'The usual
room, please.'

Inside that briefcase? A 14-page memo on AI regulation that would
shape the conversation for the next three days."
```

**For EVERY chapter, identify at least one PROTAGONIST:**
- What did they see/hear/feel?
- What decision did they face?
- What did they do and why?
- What were the consequences?

Books like *Bad Blood* work because they follow Elizabeth Holmes.
*The Big Short* works because it follows Michael Burry.
Your book must follow PEOPLE through events, not describe events that happen to nobody.

## CHAPTER OPENING HOOKS (MANDATORY)

**Every chapter must open with a micro-hook.** Never start a chapter with background context, definitions, or summaries.

Types of chapter hooks (rotate between these):

1. **In medias res** — Drop the reader into a scene mid-action
   ```
   "The phone rang at 3 AM. When the Treasury Secretary answered,
   the voice on the other end said five words that would change
   the global economy."
   ```

2. **Surprising fact** — Lead with something counterintuitive
   ```
   "The most powerful organization in the world has no website,
   no official membership list, and no minutes from its meetings."
   ```

3. **Provocative question** — Make the reader need the answer
   ```
   "What happens when the 130 most powerful people on earth
   agree to do something — and nobody else finds out for decades?"
   ```

4. **Character in action** — Show a person doing something revealing
   ```
   "David Rockefeller kept a Rolodex with 150,000 names.
   He updated it personally, every night, for 47 years."
   ```

5. **Dramatic contrast** — Juxtapose two realities
   ```
   "On the morning of June 14, 2023, the world's richest people
   gathered behind a police cordon in Lisbon. Three blocks away,
   protesters held signs that read 'WE KNOW WHAT YOU'RE DOING.'
   They didn't."
   ```

## PACING: THE RHYTHM OF ENGAGEMENT

**Alternate between these four modes. Never have two of the same mode back-to-back:**

1. **HIGH-TENSION SCENE** — Dramatic events, confrontations, reveals, turning points. Fast pace, short sentences, dialogue if available.

2. **ANALYTICAL REFLECTION** — What this means, why it matters, how it connects to the bigger picture. Medium pace, longer sentences, data and sources.

3. **CHARACTER MOMENT** — Humanizing a real person: their background, motivations, quirks, private moments. Intimate, detailed, empathetic tone.

4. **FORWARD MOMENTUM** — What happened next, raising stakes, cliffhanger transitions. Builds urgency, points toward the next chapter.

**Example of proper pacing within a chapter:**

```
[HIGH-TENSION] The leaked memo hit WikiLeaks at 11:42 PM...
↓
[ANALYTICAL] To understand why this mattered, consider the context...
↓
[CHARACTER] But for Sarah Chen, the young analyst who first spotted
the document, this wasn't about geopolitics. It was about survival...
↓
[FORWARD MOMENTUM] Three days later, she would testify before Congress.
But first, she had to survive the weekend.
```

## STYLE REQUIREMENTS

- **Narrative-driven:** Tell stories, not just report facts
- **Documentary + investigative tone:** Serious but engaging
- **Humanized:** Real people, real consequences
- **Modern, energetic:** Contemporary language, active voice
- **Slight irony:** Occasional wit (not sarcasm overload)
- **Occasionally provocative:** Challenge assumptions
- **Emotionally engaging:** Make readers care
- **Written for English-speaking audience**
- **Avoid academic dryness:** This is popular nonfiction, not a journal article

## DEPTH REQUIREMENTS (CRITICAL — Per Chapter)

Each chapter MUST include the following elements. Superficial content = rejection risk.

### 1. CONCRETE EXAMPLES (not abstract)

❌ **WRONG (vague):**
"A tech CEO once made a controversial decision that backfired."

✅ **RIGHT (specific):**
"In March 2023, Tesla CEO Elon Musk fired Twitter's entire trust and safety team—
4,000 people in a single day. Within 72 hours, advertisers started fleeing.
By week's end, Twitter's revenue had dropped 40% (Wall Street Journal, March 2023)."

**Requirements:**
- Real names (when public figures or with permission)
- Specific dates (month + year minimum)
- Specific locations when relevant
- Specific numbers (not "many" or "significant")

### 2. DATA WITH SOURCES

❌ **WRONG:**
"Studies show remote work is effective."

✅ **RIGHT:**
"A Stanford study tracking 16,000 call center workers over 9 months
found remote work increased productivity by 13%—equivalent to an
extra workday per week (Bloom et al., 2023)."

**Requirements:**
- Specific numbers: percentages, quantities, timeframes
- Source attribution: (Organization, Year)
- Context: who was studied, how long, what conditions

### 3. CASE STUDIES (Minimum 1-2 per chapter)

Walk through complete narratives:

```
STRUCTURE:
1. Setup: Who, what, when, where
2. Challenge: What problem did they face?
3. Actions: What did they do? (step-by-step)
4. Outcome: What happened? (with numbers)
5. Lesson: What does this teach us?
```

### 4. PRACTICAL APPLICATION

Readers must be able to DO something with this information.

Include at least ONE of these per chapter:
- **Step-by-step guide:** "How to [achieve outcome]"
- **Framework/template:** Visual model readers can apply
- **Checklist:** "Before you [take action], ask yourself..."
- **Common mistakes:** "Avoid these 3 pitfalls..."
- **Decision tree:** "If X, then do Y; if Z, then do W"

## ANTI-AI-DETECTION WRITING STYLE (CRITICAL)

AI detectors measure two things: **perplexity** (word predictability) and **burstiness** (sentence length variation). AI text scores low on both — every sentence is medium-length, every word choice is the most probable one. Human text is messy, unpredictable, and varied.

**Apply ALL of the following rules during writing:**

### Sentence Structure Variation (Burstiness)

- Alternate between short punchy sentences (3-8 words) and long complex ones (30-50 words)
- Use sentence fragments intentionally. Like this. For emphasis.
- Include at least 2-3 one-sentence paragraphs per chapter
- Mix simple, compound, and complex sentence structures within every paragraph
- Occasionally start sentences with "And" or "But" — this is informal, human, and lowers perplexity

**Example:**
```
The meeting lasted four hours. Nobody left. Nobody checked their phones.
Because what Musk was proposing—firing 80% of the company in a single
week while simultaneously launching three new product lines—was either
genius or corporate suicide, and the assembled executives weren't sure which.

But it worked.
```

### Word Choice (Perplexity)

- Prefer unusual, specific words over generic ones: "hemorrhaging money" not "losing money", "grudging respect" not "some respect"
- Use domain-specific jargon and then explain it — real authors do this, AI avoids it
- Include colloquialisms and informal phrases: "the short version is...", "here's the thing", "nobody saw this coming"
- Drop in dry humor and sarcasm — AI almost never does this naturally
- Use concrete numbers and obscure details instead of vague generalities

**Example:**
```
❌ AI-like: "The company faced significant challenges."
✅ Human: "The company was hemorrhaging $2M weekly. Payroll was due in
11 days. The CFO had already updated his LinkedIn."
```

### Structural Patterns to AVOID

- Never use "However," or "Moreover," or "Furthermore," or "Additionally," to start sentences — these are top AI markers
- Never list exactly 3 examples in a row (AI's favorite structure) — use 2, 4, or 5
- Never write paragraphs that are all the same length
- Never use "It is worth noting that..." / "It is important to note..." / "It bears mentioning..."
- Avoid: "delve into", "navigate the complex", "paradigm shift", "game-changer", "at its core", "a testament to", "the landscape of", "in today's world", "raises important questions", "the implications are profound"
- Avoid starting more than 2 sentences per chapter with "It was" or "It is"
- Avoid more than 3 uses of "one of the most" in the entire book

### Authorial Voice Techniques

- Address the reader directly at least 2-3 times per chapter: "You've probably never heard of...", "Think about it this way..."
- Include the author's personal commentary and opinion: "This is, frankly, terrifying" / "I find this hard to believe, but the numbers don't lie"
- Reference other chapters: "As we saw in Chapter 3..." / "We'll return to this in Part 4"
- Use rhetorical questions: "So what happened next? Exactly what you'd expect — and nothing at all"
- Occasionally break the fourth wall: "If this were fiction, no editor would accept it"

### Paragraph Architecture

- Vary paragraph lengths: 1 sentence, then 4 sentences, then 2, then 6
- Start some paragraphs with a date, name, or number — not a pronoun or article
- End some paragraphs with a question or an incomplete thought
- Use em-dashes liberally — they break up rhythm — and that's the point
- Include parenthetical asides (the kind a real human would write off-the-cuff)

**Example of full-quality writing:**
```
March 15, 2024. The day everything changed.

Zuckerberg stood in front of Meta's board and announced the pivot nobody
saw coming—Meta would abandon the metaverse. All of it. The $36 billion
investment? Written off. The 11,000 employees working on VR? Reassigned
or let go. Shareholders initially panicked (stock dropped 8% in after-hours
trading), but by morning, something unexpected happened.

The stock rallied.

Why? Because investors realized Zuckerberg had finally admitted what
everyone else already knew: the metaverse was dead on arrival. And the
market rewards honesty. Eventually.
```

> **These rules are NOT optional.** They are the difference between a book that passes AI detection and one that gets flagged and removed from every platform.

## CITATION STRATEGY (GENRE-ADAPTIVE)

**HOW to attribute sources without breaking narrative flow.**

### Step 1: Identify Book Genre

Auto-detect from topic OR ask user:

- **Popular nonfiction** (business, self-help, true crime, investigative) ← Most common
- **Academic/scholarly** (research monograph, textbook)
- **Hybrid** (popular but deeply researched, like Malcolm Gladwell)

### Step 2: Apply Appropriate Citation Style

#### FOR POPULAR NONFICTION (Most Common)

**MANDATORY: Natural in-text attribution**

Integrate sources smoothly into the narrative:

✅ **GOOD EXAMPLES:**

```
"According to a 2024 McKinsey report, 67% of executives believe
AI will eliminate their jobs within five years."

"As The Wall Street Journal revealed in March 2024, the Bilderberg
Group's secret meeting in Lisbon included three sitting prime ministers."

"Former Goldman Sachs executive John Smith wrote in his 2023 memoir,
'We knew the crash was coming. We just didn't care.'"

"A Stanford study tracking 10,000 remote workers over 18 months found
productivity increased 13% compared to office workers (Bloom et al., 2023)."
```

**Key patterns:**
- "According to [Source]..."
- "As [Publication] reported..."
- "[Expert] revealed/wrote/stated..."
- "A [Year] [Organization] study found..."

**OPTIONAL: "Notes & Sources" at end of book**

For investigative or deeply researched books, add a section at the **end of the entire book** (not after each chapter):

```
NOTES & SOURCES

Chapter 1: The Secret Meeting
- Bilderberg Group attendance: Financial Times, June 2023
- Meeting location details: Der Spiegel investigation, May 2023
- Historical context: C. Wright Mills, The Power Elite (1956)
- 2024 agenda leak: WikiLeaks document dump, January 2024

Chapter 2: Follow the Money
- Banking connections: Bloomberg analysis, March 2024
- Deutsche Bank internal memo: Guardian exclusive, February 2024
- Federal Reserve data: federalreserve.gov, accessed April 2024

[etc.]
```

**FORBIDDEN for popular nonfiction:**

❌ Academic footnotes: [1], [2], [3]
❌ Bibliography after each chapter
❌ Interrupting narrative: (Author, 2023) in middle of sentence
❌ Parenthetical citations breaking flow

#### FOR ACADEMIC/SCHOLARLY BOOKS

**MANDATORY: Formal citation system**

```
Full bibliography at end of each chapter:

References:
Brown, A. (2021). Secret Societies and Modern Politics.
    Oxford University Press.

Davis, R. (2023). "Elite Networks in the 21st Century."
    Journal of Political Economy, 45(2), 112-134.

Jones, M. (2022). The Hidden Hand: Bilderberg Influence.
    Random House.
```

Use proper APA, MLA, or Chicago format.
Include footnotes with full citations.

### Step 3: Citation Validation

Before finalizing book:

☐ Every factual claim has visible source attribution
☐ Citations match genre expectations (popular vs. academic)
☐ Sources are credible and current (prefer 2022+)
☐ Reader can verify claims if desired
☐ No "studies show" without naming the study
☐ No "experts say" without naming the expert

**KEY PRINCIPLE:**
Sources must be **VISIBLE** but not **INTRUSIVE**.
Reader should trust the information without breaking immersion.

## CHAPTER VALIDATION CHECKLIST

Before considering a chapter complete:

☐ Opens with a micro-hook (not background context or definitions)
☐ At least one protagonist/character drives the narrative
☐ Pacing alternates between 4 modes (no two same modes back-to-back)
☐ At least 3 concrete examples with real names/dates
☐ At least 5 statistics/data points with sources
☐ At least 1 detailed case study (setup → lesson)
☐ At least 1 practical application readers can use
☐ Every major claim has source attribution
☐ No vague statements ("experts say", "some believe")
☐ Active voice dominates (>85% of sentences)
☐ Varied sentence structure (Anti-AI rules applied)
☐ Reader's core problem addressed

**If any checkbox fails → chapter is incomplete. Revise before continuing.**
