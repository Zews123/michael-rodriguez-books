# INTRODUCTION — The Clock Nobody Wanted to Read

In the basement of Building E52 at MIT, sometime in the autumn of 1971, a computer the size of a small bedroom was grinding through equations that would make most economists lose sleep for the next half century. Seventeen researchers — professors, systems dynamicists, economists, one philosopher — had been given a single question by a shadowy group of European industrialists known as the Club of Rome: "How long can human civilization continue to grow on a finite planet?"

They built a model. Called it World3. Fed it five variables: population, food production, industrial output, pollution, and non-renewable resources. The simulation peaked somewhere around the 2020s and 2030s, then bent downward. By 2040, the decline was unmistakable. They published in 1972 under the title *The Limits to Growth*. It sold thirty million copies. And then — because this is how humans work — almost everyone decided to ignore it.

Fast forward to 2020. Gaya Herrington — not an activist, but a sustainability analyst at KPMG — sat down with the original equations and overlaid fifty years of real-world data on the 1972 projections. The curves matched. The trajectory lined up with disturbing precision against the scenario labeled "Business As Usual 2." The collapse scenario. She published in the *Yale Journal of Industrial Ecology* in 2021. It went viral. And then most people scrolled past it and went back to arguing about cryptocurrency.

## Chapter 1: The Graveyard of Greatness

Twelve thousand years of civilization. For every one that exists today, dozens have vanished so completely we don't even know their names. Historians argue viciously about why, but the broad consensus lands on four categories: political dysfunction, social erosion, environmental degradation, economic failure. The same four failure modes have killed every dead civilization on the planet.

### The Bronze Age Collapse — When Everybody Fell at Once

Around 1200 BCE, within roughly fifty years, nearly every major civilization in the eastern Mediterranean collapsed. The Mycenaean Greeks. The Hittites. Egypt (survived but permanently diminished). Drought hammered agriculture. Trade networks snapped. The mysterious "Sea Peoples" raided coastal cities. No single cause. A cascade. Eric Cline calls it "a systems collapse" — multiple stressors hitting an interconnected network simultaneously, each one making the others worse.

### The Maya — A Civilization That Ate Itself

Severe droughts hit the Yucatán in the 9th century, but the Maya had survived droughts before. What was different: centuries of deforestation had degraded the soil and reduced rainfall. Warfare between city-states had intensified. Rulers built ever-larger monuments while the labor force wasn't producing food. Within fifty years — a single lifetime — the southern lowland cities were abandoned. The population dropped by 90% in some regions.

### Easter Island — Collapse in a Petri Dish

The islanders consumed the resource base that sustained their civilization. They could see it happening — the island is small enough that the deforestation would have been visible to everyone — and they did it anyway. Jared Diamond asks the question that haunts every page of this book: "What was the person who cut down the last tree thinking?" Scale that logic up to eight billion people sharing a planet with finite resources, and you have the premise of the World3 model.

## Chapter 2: The Greatest Civilization That Ever Lived

Imagine two lives. Door one: a modest apartment in St. Petersburg. Below-average income. Smartphone, internet, supermarket, clinic. Door two: a genuine fifteenth-century aristocrat. A hundred hectares, a stone castle, servants, your own army. But water comes from the river, medicine doesn't exist, a toothache can kill you, and life expectancy is forty-five.

You'd pick the apartment. That gap tells you something about the exponential nature of civilizational progress. We haven't improved by 10% over five centuries. We've improved by factors of thousands. Nobody fights to protect something they don't understand they have.

### The Jet Fighter and the Camel

Our modern global economy is a jet fighter — astonishing performance, breathtaking capability, zero redundancy. One tiny malfunction and you're falling out of the sky. A camel gets sick, recovers, eats some thorns, and keeps walking. We optimized for efficiency, not resilience. The market selects for fragility. In March 2021, one ship stuck sideways in the Suez Canal cost global trade $9.6 billion per day. In February 2021, a cold snap brought Texas — GDP larger than Australia's — to its knees for a week.

COVID-19 didn't destroy infrastructure. It just slowed things down. Result: $22 trillion hit to global GDP, supply chain chaos lasting years. TSMC produces 90% of the world's most advanced chips. One company. On an island 100 miles off a superpower that claims it as sovereign territory. If that doesn't strike you as a systemic risk, I'm not sure what would.
