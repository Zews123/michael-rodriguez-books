# A 1972 Model Predicted Decline by 2040

In 2021, KPMG sustainability analyst Gaya Herrington published a peer-reviewed study in the *Yale Journal of Industrial Ecology*. She had taken the original 1972 MIT World3 model — the one behind *The Limits to Growth*, a study that sold 30 million copies in 30 languages before being largely dismissed as Cold War pessimism — and overlaid fifty years of real-world data onto its projections.

The result: current empirical data tracks the "Business As Usual 2" scenario. That's the collapse trajectory.

This isn't fringe science. This is a Fortune-500 consultancy analyst publishing in a Yale-affiliated journal, using publicly available data sets that anyone can verify. And the professional implications deserve serious attention from anyone making decisions with a time horizon beyond the next quarterly report.

## Three Data Points Worth Your Time

**1. The model's variables are boardroom variables.**

The World3 model tracks five inputs: population, food production, industrial output, pollution, and non-renewable resources. These aren't abstract academic constructs — they're the same variables that drive supply chain strategy, commodity pricing, infrastructure planning, and long-term capital allocation. The model projects industrial output peaking in the 2020s–2030s, then entering a sustained decline. If you're making 10- to 15-year investment decisions, underwriting infrastructure projects, or building supply chain resilience, this trajectory is directly relevant to your risk calculus.

**2. Systemic fragility is already visible in the data.**

The 2008 financial crisis. The 2020 supply chain collapse. The 2021 Texas grid failure that left millions without power in the wealthiest country on Earth. The 2022 fertilizer shock that threatened food production across multiple continents. Each was treated as an isolated event — bad luck, poor regulation, an outlier. Viewed through the lens of systems dynamics, they form a pattern — one that is disturbingly consistent with what happens when complex systems approach the limits of their carrying capacity. Anthropologist Joseph Tainter's framework on the diminishing returns of complexity provides the theoretical scaffolding: civilizations don't fail from a single shock; they fail when the cost of maintaining complexity exceeds its benefits. The parallels to modern supply chain architecture, financial interdependencies, and energy infrastructure are hard to ignore.

**3. Historical precedent is sobering.**

The Bronze Age Collapse around 1200 BCE destroyed every major eastern Mediterranean civilization within fifty years — not a single-cause failure, but a systemic cascade of interconnected collapses across trade, agriculture, and governance. Across 12,000 years of recorded civilization, dozens of complex societies have vanished entirely, often after long periods of apparent stability and prosperity. The assumption that modern industrial civilization is exempt from this pattern — that our technology, our institutions, our global networks make us fundamentally different — is precisely that: an untested assumption. And the data from the World3 model suggests it may be wrong.
