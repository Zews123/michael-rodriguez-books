<h1>The 1972 Computer Model That Saw 2040 Coming</h1>

<p>In 1972, seventeen scientists at MIT loaded five variables into a PDP-10 computer — a machine with less processing power than the phone in your pocket. Population. Food production. Industrial output. Pollution. Non-renewable resources. The model's verdict: civilizational decline beginning around 2040.</p>

<p>Their study became <em>The Limits to Growth</em>. It sold 30 million copies in 30 languages. Then the world forgot about it.</p>

<h2>The Data Caught Up</h2>

<p>In 2021, KPMG sustainability analyst Gaya Herrington overlaid fifty years of real-world data onto the original 1972 projections. Her findings, published in the <em>Yale Journal of Industrial Ecology</em>, sent a chill through the research community: the real-world data tracks the "Business As Usual 2" scenario — the one the original team had labeled the <strong>collapse trajectory</strong>.</p>

<p>The model shows industrial output peaking in the 2020s and 2030s, then declining. Not a sudden Hollywood catastrophe — a slow, systemic unraveling driven by compounding pressures: resource depletion, pollution accumulation, and the diminishing returns of complexity.</p>

<h2>We've Seen the Previews</h2>

<p>Consider the signals we've already received:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>2008:</strong> A financial crisis that nearly collapsed the global banking system.</li>
<li><strong>2020:</strong> A supply chain breakdown that exposed the fragility of interconnected global systems.</li>
<li><strong>2021:</strong> A cold snap knocked out the Texas power grid for millions — in the world's wealthiest country.</li>
<li><strong>2022:</strong> A fertilizer shock threatening food production across entire continents.</li>
</ul>

<p>These weren't random bad luck. Systems dynamics tells us they're early tremors of what happens when complex civilizations approach the limits of their carrying capacity. Anthropologist Joseph Tainter documented this pattern across 12,000 years: civilizations collapse not from a single blow, but from the diminishing returns on the complexity they build to solve problems.</p>

<h2>Three Futures, One Choice</h2>

<p>In <em>Countdown to Collapse: The 2040 Crisis</em>, independent researcher Michael Rodriguez examines the original MIT model, Herrington's 2021 validation, and real-world case studies — from China's demographic cliff to the American Southwest water wars to Denmark's energy revolution. He outlines three scenarios: technological salvation, conscious correction, and business as usual.</p>

<p>Two require action. One requires nothing — and it's the path we're currently on.</p>

<p>The window is closing. The data doesn't wait for consensus.</p>

## FACEBOOK

You've probably never heard of the World3 model. That's kind of the problem.

In 1972, seventeen scientists at MIT built a computer simulation to project the future of civilization. They tracked five variables — population, food production, industrial output, pollution, and non-renewable resources — and the model said we'd start declining around 2040.

The study, *The Limits to Growth*, sold 30 million copies. It was translated into 30 languages. Then the world collectively shrugged and moved on.

Fast forward to 2021. KPMG analyst Gaya Herrington overlaid fifty years of actual data onto the original projections. Her paper, published in the *Yale Journal of Industrial Ecology*, found that we're tracking the "Business As Usual 2" scenario. That's the collapse trajectory.

Not the optimistic one. Not the stabilization one. The bad one.

Michael Rodriguez's *Countdown to Collapse: The 2040 Crisis* digs into the original model, the 2021 validation, and the real-world fractures already showing — from supply chain breakdowns to water wars to demographic cliffs. He lays out three scenarios and makes a case for why the window for the better ones is closing fast.
